Analysis

Oil Is at $83. Yields Are Still Rising. The Bond Market Is Telling You What the Oil Market Decline Is Not.

WTI has fallen from $119.43 to $83.77 in two sessions, a 30% collapse from the conflict high. In a normal cycle, a 30% oil decline would produce a powerful bond rally as inflation expectations repriced lower. US 10Y yields are rising. US 2Y yields are rising. US 30Y yields are rising. The entire yield curve is moving in the wrong direction for the relief narrative. This is the single most important signal in this morning's data, and it arrives six hours before the February CPI print that the entire market is treating as the week's defining data point. The bond market already knows what February CPI will say. It has decided that February is irrelevant.

Open Range Capital Research · Marcus Drauschke · March 11, 2026

The diagnostic logic of the current bond market behavior requires careful accounting. When oil falls sharply, inflation expectations normally fall with it, yields decline, and the bond market rallies. This is the standard transmission mechanism of energy prices into fixed income. It is not happening. WTI fell from $107 to $89 on Day 10 and yields fell modestly. WTI fell further to $83.77 overnight and yields are rising. The standard transmission mechanism has broken. What the bond market is pricing in its place is the inflation path that the Iran conflict has already locked in regardless of where oil settles today. Iraq has cut 70% of its production. Saudi Aramco has shut in fields. Ras Tanura, Bapco, and Ras Laffan are producing nothing. These structural supply impairments take weeks to months to reverse even after a ceasefire. The oil price can fall because Trump said the war is "very complete, pretty much." The physical supply of refined energy products that those facilities produce cannot recover because of a statement to CBS News. The bond market knows the difference. Yields are rising because the inflation path for March, April, and May is not determined by where WTI trades on March 11. It is determined by whether Gulf refining capacity comes back online and when.

The Japan 40Y yield at 3.676% is the second structural signal in this morning's data. Japan's 10Y yield fell slightly to 2.162%, which looks like relief. The 40Y rising to a new crisis high simultaneously is the opposite of relief. A steepening long end in Japan at these levels is the bond market pricing a sustained inflationary environment that the Bank of Japan cannot address without inflicting severe pain on the domestic economy. Japan imports 100% of its oil. A sustained period of oil above $80 adds directly to import costs, weakens the yen, and adds to domestic inflation. The BoJ's response options are: raise rates to defend the yen and the long end, which crushes growth; or maintain current policy and watch the curve steepen and the yen weaken further. The 40Y at 3.676% is the market assigning meaningful probability to the second outcome persisting. USD/JPY at 157.99 is creeping back toward the 158-160 intervention threshold that the BoJ has historically defended. The 40Y and USD/JPY are pricing the same scenario from different angles.

The DAX's +2.39% Tuesday session is the most aggressive single-day European equity rally since the conflict began, and it is already partially reversing in futures at -0.27%. The explanation for both the rally and the overnight fade is the same: European equity is pricing oil. DAX composition is heavy in energy-intensive industrials, auto manufacturers, and chemical companies. When oil falls from $107 to $83, the margin relief for those sectors is real and immediate. The equity market captured it in a single session. The overnight futures fade reflects the recognition that the margin relief stops there. Heating Oil is +0.35% this morning. Natural Gas is -0.13% but remains well above pre-conflict levels. Refined products and LNG, which European industry actually buys, are not following crude lower at the same rate. German pump prices are approaching the pre-conflict all-time record. The ADAC national average was already above €1.90 at the beginning of last week. The relationship between WTI's spot price and what European households pay at the forecourt runs through the refinery capacity that is currently offline.

February CPI at 08:30 ET today is the week's most anticipated scheduled data point and simultaneously the data point with the most limited forward information content. The consensus is +0.3% month-on-month and +2.4% to +2.5% year-on-year on headline, with core at +0.3% month-on-month and +2.5% year-on-year. January CPI came in at +2.4% YoY and +2.5% core, the lowest core reading since April 2021, driven by declining used car prices, falling gasoline, and cooling shelter. February data was collected entirely before February 28. The gasoline component of February CPI will show the pre-conflict price, which had been declining. The shelter component will reflect the January and early February rental and OER trend, which was already cooling. The number is, by construction, incapable of telling the market anything meaningful about the inflation path that begins with oil at $83 sustained by refinery force majeures and structural Gulf production shut-ins. Every analyst covering this release has said as much. The market knows. The bond market has already said it does not care. The equity market will trade the number anyway, because it is the only scheduled catalyst available before the FOMC next week.

The divergence between WTI and the US yield curve is the structural signal of the week. Energy economists at ClearBridge noted this week that February's CPI data is "from before the recent conflict" and that the oil shock effect is "going to be a March and April dynamic." The bond market has already assigned that forward path. It is not waiting for the data. The 10Y at 4.147% on a day when oil is at $83 and equity markets are broadly flat reflects a yield curve that has internalized the structural inflation problem independently of the daily crude print. The relief at the oil level is genuine. The transmission of that relief into bond prices is broken because the supply-side damage is not located in the barrel price. It is located in refinery availability, Gulf production capacity, and the fertilizer and LNG supply chain disruptions that propagate into food and heating prices over a three to six month lag. None of those transmission channels are addressed by WTI trading at $83.

The practical implication for portfolio positioning is direct: the oil relief trade in equities has a ceiling set by the yield curve's refusal to confirm it. An equity rally that is not supported by falling yields and a recovering bond market is not a durable rally. It is a single-variable repricing of the oil input cost. Once that repricing is complete, the second variable reasserts: the cost of capital is not falling, private credit is gating, and NFP already came in at -92,000 in a world where oil averaged $65-70. The full cost-of-capital and growth impact of the Iran shock has not yet appeared in any scheduled data release. It will first appear in March PMI, April NFP, and April CPI. These are the numbers that will actually matter. Today's CPI is the last backward-looking print before the FOMC must act.

Three Theses · What the Data Is Actually Saying
Thesis I · The Yield Signal
Oil Fell 30% From the Peak. Yields Rose. When the Standard Transmission Mechanism Breaks, the Bond Market Is Pricing Something With a Longer Duration Than the Current Oil Price.

The relationship between energy prices and bond yields is one of the most reliable transmission channels in macroeconomics. When oil falls, inflation expectations fall, yields fall, bonds rally. The current data shows oil at $83 and all three US yield tenors rising. This breakdown is not noise. It is the bond market's declaration that the inflation problem in this cycle is not located in the barrel price of crude, but in the structural supply impairment that will persist for months regardless of where WTI trades on any given day. Iraq has cut 70% of production. Saudi Aramco has shut in fields. Three major Gulf refinery and LNG complexes are under force majeure. These are not factors that normalize when Trump says the war is "very complete." They normalize when operational conditions on the ground permit restart, which is a physical timeline measured in weeks to months, not in statements. The bond market has decided that the inflation path for March through at least June is already determined. It is pricing that path through rising yields even as oil retreats. This is the correct read.

Thesis II · Europe's Asymmetric Exposure
DAX +2.39% Tuesday. DAX Futures -0.27% Wednesday. The Single-Variable Repricing Is Already Complete. What Remains Is the Structural Problem.

The DAX's 2.39% Tuesday session captured the full oil input cost relief trade for European industrials and auto manufacturers in a single session. The overnight fade in futures reflects the second-order reality: European energy exposure does not stop at crude oil. Germany imports 100% of its oil and the majority of its gas. German pump prices stood at approximately €1.97 per liter on March 9, approaching the March 2022 all-time record of €2.259. Individual stations in Bremen and other cities have already crossed €2.00. The EU average is €1.574. The DAX rally priced WTI at $83. It has not yet priced the refinery capacity that is offline, the LNG force majeures that constrain European gas supply, or the heating oil trajectory that is +0.35% today against crude's mild positive. The ECB is in the same policy trap as the Fed without the dollar's reserve currency buffer. European sovereign bonds, represented by the Bund at essentially flat, are not participating in the equity rally. That divergence between DAX equities and Bund fixed income is the same signal as the US yield curve rising against falling WTI. Both markets are telling you the problem has a longer tail than the relief trade implies.

Thesis III · CPI's Asymmetric Reaction Function
Hot Print Lasts. Cool Print Is Moot. The Market Knows February Is Backward-Looking. The Bond Market Has Already Moved to March.

The February CPI consensus is +2.5% YoY headline and +0.3% core month-on-month. January CPI was +2.4% YoY with +2.5% core, the lowest core reading since April 2021. February was collected entirely before the Iran conflict. The energy component will show pre-conflict gasoline prices, which were falling. Shelter is expected to continue cooling. This is the last backward-looking inflation print the FOMC will see before its March 17-18 decision. The market's reaction function is asymmetric: a hot print produces durable bond selling because it adds pre-conflict stickiness to the Iran-shock trajectory, compounding the problem. A cool print produces brief equity relief but cannot be sustained because every participant knows March CPI, reported in mid-April, will show the full weight of $100+ oil average for an entire month. The FOMC must make a rate decision next week against a -92,000 NFP, a yield curve that refuses to rally on oil relief, and a CPI print that cannot tell them what they actually need to know about the March inflation path. There is no good option in the dot plot. Whatever Powell says on March 18 is the most consequential central bank communication since Jackson Hole 2022.
