This brief has tracked the stagflation thesis since Sunday March 2, when oil first spiked and US Treasury yields rose rather than fell, the signature of supply-side inflation pricing rather than growth-shock safety buying. Every day since, the bond market has added confirmation: US 10Y from sub-4% to 4.196%, UK Gilt down seven consecutive sessions, Japan 10Y rising for six sessions straight to 2.188%. Friday's NFP result was not a surprise to fixed income. It was the labor market confirming what the bond market already knew. The economy was losing jobs in February before oil reached $80. It is at $107 now and accelerating toward the price level that Morgan Stanley and JPMorgan had identified as the threshold for global growth disruption. The sequence is complete. The data has arrived at the conclusion the bond market pre-priced nine days ago.

The NFP internals matter as much as the headline. Negative 92,000 is not a rounding error or a weather-related distortion. Private payrolls were negative. The unemployment rate rose to 4.4% against an expectation of 4.3%. The prior two months were revised downward. This is a genuine labor market contraction driven by the tariff-shock slowdown that was visible in PMI data throughout January and February, compounding with DOGE-related federal employment reductions that had already removed government sector jobs from the count. The number captures February, before a barrel of WTI cost $107. The March labor market, when it reports in April, will show the oil shock cost channel layered on top of the existing tariff slowdown. The NFP trajectory from January's +126,000 revised to -92,000 is the steepest two-month deterioration in the labor market since the pandemic's onset. The Fed has one mandate being violated by oil and another being violated by jobs. Its policy rate is doing nothing useful for either.

The BlackRock gating event is the development that most market commentary is treating as a footnote and should be treating as a lead paragraph. This is not Blue Owl gating a niche private credit vehicle or New Mountain Finance restricting access to a smaller fund. BlackRock is the world's largest asset manager, managing more than $10 trillion. A gating event at BlackRock signals that redemption pressure in private credit has reached a scale where even the deepest-pocketed managers cannot absorb it through normal liquidity management. The progression over the past three weeks has been textbook contagion: smaller specialist funds (Tricolor Financial, Blue Owl), then mid-tier managers (New Mountain), and now the largest institution in the space. Each gating event has two effects. It confirms that the redemption pressure is structural, not idiosyncratic, and it encourages other investors in other funds to submit redemption requests before they too are gated. This dynamic, once started, does not stop on its own.

The private credit linkage to the Iran conflict is direct and underappreciated. Private credit, as documented in Part II of this publication's SaaS disruption research, has concentrated approximately 40% of sponsor-backed loans in software and technology companies. Software stocks are down 23% year-to-date. AI disruption risk is repricing the enterprises that private lenders have financed. Separately, oil at $107 is compressing the operating margins of every leveraged borrower in energy-intensive sectors. Rates are rising, not falling, because the Fed cannot cut. Defaults are beginning to move. The Ares Management, Blackstone, KKR, and Apollo stocks are down more than 20% year-to-date according to Sherwood's reporting, which is the equity market pricing exactly this sequence. The gating events are the private credit market's equivalent of those stock prices: the realization catching up to the projection.

Qatar's energy minister giving a $150 oil target with a two-to-three-week timeline is the most significant official statement since Trump's Truth Social escort pledge. The Qatari statement has the advantage of being operationally grounded: Qatar runs one of the largest LNG production operations in the world and is itself exposed to Hormuz risk as both exporter and host of US military assets at Al Udeid Air Base. When Qatar's energy minister says $150 in two to three weeks, he is making a supply-chain forecast from direct knowledge of Hormuz flow conditions, not a geopolitical headline grab. That statement moved the intraday WTI price to $119.43 on Friday. The settlement at $107 reflects relief that a $119 print did not hold, not any structural improvement in the supply picture. WTI at $107 is still the highest print since July 2022 and is now 55% above the pre-conflict level of approximately $69.

The Event That Is Being Underpriced · BlackRock Private Credit Gating
BlackRock · Flagship Private Credit Fund · Gating Event · Week of March 6, 2026

BlackRock has restricted withdrawals from one of its flagship private credit funds following a surge in redemption requests. The fund joins Blue Owl and New Mountain Finance, which gate earlier in this period. This is not a sequence of isolated events. It is a contagion pattern progressing from specialist funds to mid-tier managers to the largest asset manager in the world, in a three-week window.

Private credit's structural vulnerability has three concurrent sources in this environment. First: approximately 40% of sponsor-backed loans in the space are tied to software and technology companies. Software stocks are down 23% year-to-date on AI disruption fears, compressing the enterprise values of the underlying borrowers. Second: oil at $107 is crushing operating margins across energy-intensive leveraged borrowers. Third: rates are rising into a growth slowdown, the worst possible refinancing environment for leveraged credit portfolios.

The gating dynamic is self-reinforcing. Each redemption restriction triggers inquiry from investors in other funds: if BlackRock is gating, what does that tell me about my exposure elsewhere? Ares Management, Blackstone, KKR, and Apollo are all down more than 20% YTD. The equity market is already pricing this cascade. The private credit market itself, by its nature illiquid and opaque, is just beginning to recognize it. This is the credit-market dimension of the Iran shock and it has no direct connection to the Strait of Hormuz. It is the oil price transmission mechanism through corporate balance sheets into the capital markets infrastructure that finances most of the leveraged US economy.

Investment Theses · Three Frameworks for the Week Ahead
Thesis I · The US 2Y Did Not Rally on -92,000 NFP. That Is the Most Important Data Point of the Week.
The Short End of the Curve Rising on a Catastrophic Jobs Miss Means: The Bond Market Believes Oil Inflation Overrides the Growth Mandate.

In any normal economic environment, a -92,000 NFP print, the worst result since 2020 and 151,000 below expectations, would produce a sharp rally in short-term Treasuries as the market priced aggressive Fed cuts in response to a collapsing labor market. That is not what happened Friday. The US 2Y yield rose 5.9 basis points to 3.615% on the day the economy lost 92,000 jobs. The bond market looked at a jobs disaster and decided: it does not matter, oil at $100+ means the Fed cannot cut, and the 2Y reflects that constraint.

This is the clearest possible articulation of the policy trap. The Fed's dual mandate has never been more simultaneously violated: maximum employment is deteriorating at its fastest pace since the pandemic, and price stability is being threatened by the largest single-week energy price surge since the 1970s. Standard policy logic would have these pulling in the same direction: when jobs are lost, cut rates. The current configuration has them pointing in opposite directions: cutting rates when oil is at $107 imports inflation, sends the dollar lower, and risks a 1970s energy-wage spiral. Not cutting when employment is contracting accepts the recession. The 2Y at 3.615% rising on a -92,000 NFP is the bond market's verdict: it believes the Fed will prioritize inflation, hold rates, and accept the employment deterioration. That verdict is correct. And it means the equity market has not yet fully priced the earnings implications of a Fed-hold into an oil-shock recession.

Thesis II · The Gilt Is the Week's Most Honest Asset
UK Gilt -7.4% Cumulative Over Seven Sessions. No Other Major Sovereign Bond Has Moved This Much. It Is Telling You Everything.

The UK Gilt's cumulative seven-session decline of approximately 7.4% from pre-crisis levels is the most extreme single-asset move in this entire crisis, larger than the S&P drawdown, larger than gold's ATH-to-current pullback, and more consistent than oil's volatile intraday swings. The Gilt is the cleanest expression of the stagflation thesis because the UK has all three vulnerabilities simultaneously: it imports most of its oil and gas, it runs a current account deficit, and it has an already-strained fiscal position under Chancellor Reeves's Spring Statement framework with limited room for additional borrowing.

The Bank of England's position mirrors the Fed's exactly: it cannot cut rates into an oil shock that is producing CPI above target, and it cannot raise rates into a labor market deterioration and a growth slowdown. Its only genuine option is to hold and absorb the pressure, which is what the Gilt market is pricing: a central bank that is frozen while inflation runs and growth decelerates. Sterling at 1.3321 and falling reflects the same logic from the currency side. The UK's current account deficit means it needs to attract foreign capital to fund itself; a frozen central bank in a stagflationary environment is not an attractive proposition for foreign bond buyers. The Gilt tells the full story of how a sovereign bond market reprices when its central bank has no good options. Every other major sovereign market is following it, just more slowly.

Thesis III · The Agricultural Dimension No One Is Discussing
Wheat +2.62%. Soybeans +1.66%. The Hormuz Closure Is a Fertilizer Supply Crisis During Planting Season.

The Hormuz closure is being reported as an oil and gas crisis. It is also a fertilizer crisis arriving at the worst possible seasonal moment. The Strait of Hormuz is a transit corridor not only for crude oil and LNG but for potash, phosphate, and ammonia, the three primary fertilizer ingredients that flow from Gulf production hubs to global agriculture. Planting season in the Northern Hemisphere begins in March and April. Fertilizer that does not reach fields in the next six to eight weeks does not produce the crop that feeds into autumn harvest prices. The wheat and soybean moves in this morning's data are not driven by normal commodity trading dynamics; they are driven by the beginning of the agricultural supply chain pricing the same Hormuz disruption that oil markets have been pricing all week.

CF Industries, Bunge, and ADM were all flagged last week as fertilizer-related beneficiaries of the disruption. This thesis has legs into the second quarter. If Hormuz remains effectively closed through April, the fertilizer supply shortfall will produce a second inflationary wave in food prices that arrives in the CPI data approximately six months after the energy shock. The first inflation wave from oil will show up in March and April CPI. The second wave from food will show up in September and October. The Fed will be managing two sequential inflation shocks separated by six months, on top of a labor market that is already contracting. That is the multi-quarter inflation path that 1970s comparisons are built on. The wheat price this morning is a leading indicator for that path.

The Week Ahead · What Resolves and What Does Not

Wednesday's CPI Is the First Scheduled Policy Catalyst. The FOMC Is the Last.

Wednesday's February CPI release is the most important scheduled economic data point of the week. The number captures February inflation, which includes the tariff effect but only the very beginning of the oil shock (oil was at $65-70 in February before the strikes). A hot February CPI confirms the pre-existing inflation problem. A cold February CPI would be moot because the oil shock has already sent the March and April trajectory sharply higher regardless of what February showed. The market knows this, which means the CPI's market impact is asymmetric: a hot print causes immediate bond selling and equity pressure; a cold print provides only brief relief because the trajectory is visible.

The March 17-18 FOMC is now the most consequential scheduled macro event of the quarter. Powell must simultaneously acknowledge a -92,000 jobs print, $107 WTI, and a 5-year breakeven inflation expectation already rising through 2.46%, with no policy tool that addresses all three. The dot plot will show the Fed's projection of where rates go in 2026. Those dots were set based on pre-conflict economic data. Powell will need to explain, in real time at the press conference, how the Fed thinks about a supply-side inflation shock that it cannot cut through while growth is contracting and private credit markets are beginning to gate. There is no script for that press conference. Whatever Powell says, it will be the most scrutinized central bank communication since the 2022 Jackson Hole speech that began the fastest rate-hiking cycle in forty years.

The economy lost 92,000 jobs. Oil is at $107. The Fed is frozen. BlackRock is gating. Qatar is warning of $150. The bond market priced all of this before the data arrived. The equity market is catching up this morning.
