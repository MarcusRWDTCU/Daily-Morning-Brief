Lead Story ¬∑ Tuesday Feb 24

15% and Rising. Software in Free Fall. Nvidia on Deck.

Trump escalates to 15% global tariffs over the weekend. Software stocks enter a full bear market on AI disruption fears. And Wednesday's Nvidia print may be the most consequential earnings release of the year. The market has a lot to process.

Monday was ugly. The Dow shed 822 points (-1.66%), the S&P 500 fell 1.04% to 6,837, back in the red for 2026, and the Nasdaq lost 1.13%. The twin drivers: tariff re-escalation and the so-called "SaaSpocalypse." On Saturday, Trump posted on Truth Social raising his new 10% global tariff immediately to 15%, warning that countries trying to "play games" would face even higher levies. The pattern is now unmistakable: every legal check produces a stronger executive counterpunch.

The software sector is in genuine distress. IBM dropped 13% on Monday after Anthropic detailed new coding capabilities in its Claude Code product. Microsoft fell 3%, CrowdStrike retreated nearly 10%. Citrini Research's weekend note arguing that AI will hollow out white-collar labor faster than markets appreciate, has become the most-circulated sellside piece in months. Read our take on this: The Structural Revaluation in Application Software and Part 2: Blue Owl and the Credit Market Dimension. Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang called the software bear case "the most illogical thing in the world." He gets his chance to back that up on Wednesday after close.

02 The Macro Picture Global

Tariffs Trump's 15% global tariff is now in effect, replacing the 10% announced just days after the Supreme Court struck down the IEEPA framework. The EU has requested full clarity on the new regime and warned it could jeopardize transatlantic trade deals. The trajectory is unmistakable: every legal constraint produces escalation, not retreat. The Section 122 framework under the Trade Act of 1974 gives the president unilateral authority, valid for only 150 days without Congressional approval. That clock is now running, and markets have not priced that deadline.

Key Structural Read ¬∑ The 150-Day Clock

Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 authorizes tariffs up to 15% for 150 days without Congress. Day one started this past weekend. By late July 2026, either Congress ratifies these tariffs or the authority expires automatically. This binary event is not in any Wall Street scenario deck I have seen, and it may be the single most important structural variable in U.S. trade policy for the next six months.

Gold / Silver Gold reached $5,225 intraday on Monday, sitting roughly 3.2% below its all-time high of $5,364 set on January 29. Overnight it has pulled back to $5,190 (-0.68%). Silver is up 12% in five sessions and still well below its January high. The precious metals complex is acting as the primary safe-haven as both the dollar and bonds fail in their traditional roles. With DXY near 97 and yields falling on risk-off flows, gold is filling the vacuum left by a reserve currency whose issuer is the source of the crisis.

SaaSpocalypse Software stocks have entered a bear market. IBM -13%, CrowdStrike -10%, Microsoft -3% in a single session. Citrini Research's weekend note on AI labor displacement has gone viral institutionally. The counterargument from Jensen Huang, arguing that AI creates more software demand not less, gets its most important empirical test on Wednesday when Nvidia reports. The data center revenue guidance is the number that matters above all others.

Rates Yields fell Monday on risk-off flows: 10-year down 6 bps to 4.01%, 5-year down 7 bps to 3.57%. The bond market is re-pricing a scenario where growth softens faster than inflation. Fed cut expectations remain suppressed ¬∑ June at 57% probability is the earliest live meeting. Three Fed speakers today (Goolsbee, Bostic, Cook) deliver their first commentary since tariff escalation to 15%. Watch for any shift in the "inflation vs. growth" framing.

Open Range Research Perspective

The market is pricing two shocks simultaneously: tariff cost inflation and AI labor displacement. They point in opposite directions for monetary policy: inflation argues for the Fed to hold, AI-driven deflation argues for cuts. This contradiction is the defining tension of 2026. Until it resolves, expect elevated VIX, a bid for gold, and rotation away from software into energy, defense, and physical infrastructure. The resolution likely arrives in two stages: Nvidia earnings Wednesday, then the Fed's April meeting. In that order.

05 Three Theses for Today Research

1. The 150-Day Tariff Clock is the Most Underpriced Risk in Markets. Section 122 gives Trump 15% tariff authority for 150 days without Congress. Markets are pricing these tariffs as permanent. They are not, legally. The resolution, either Congressional ratification or expiry by late July, is a massive binary that does not appear in any Wall Street scenario analysis. Long volatility into July is an asymmetric trade.

2. Gold is the New Dollar. DXY near 97, yields falling on risk-off, equities selling off, and gold holding above $5,190, only 3.2% below its January 29 all-time high of $5,364. The dollar is no longer functioning as the primary safe-haven in a crisis the U.S. itself is generating. Central banks globally have been accumulating gold since 2022. This is structural, not cyclical. The playbook is not changing.

3. Nvidia is the Fulcrum. If Nvidia delivers on Wednesday: $65B+ revenue, strong Blackwell guidance, constructive China commentary, it severs the link between AI capex concerns and equity weakness. If it disappoints or guides cautiously, the SaaSpocalypse narrative gets a fresh catalyst and the tech selloff broadens into hardware. This is not a normal earnings event. It is a referendum on the entire AI investment thesis.
