Analysis

Iran Hit a Tanker. The Trump Escort Pledge Met Its First Real Test on Day 6 and a Ship Was Struck. NFP Lands at 08:30.

Thursday provided two facts that define this morning's setup. First: Iran fired a missile at an oil tanker in the Gulf, WTI crossed $80, and the Dow fell 1,100 points intraday before partially recovering. The escort pledge, now four days old, had not prevented the first actual attack on shipping. Second: February Non-Farm Payrolls print at 08:30 ET, with a consensus of 58k-65k, half of January's number. In this environment, both a strong and a weak NFP are bad for markets in different ways. European futures are green overnight. Oil is slightly off its highs. The week's final session opens with the most consequential data release of the entire crisis on top of an already-destabilised macro backdrop.

Open Range Capital Research · Marcus Drauschke · March 6, 2026

The tanker attack on Thursday is the most significant escalation since Monday's drone strikes on the US Embassy in Riyadh. The distinction matters: drones hitting a diplomatic compound is a political statement. A missile hitting a commercial oil vessel is an economic attack. It is the first direct test of whether the Trump escort commitment can function as a deterrent in an active conflict zone, and the answer the market received was: not yet. Hundreds of ships remain stuck in the Persian Gulf according to Reuters. The Navy escort infrastructure, which as noted in Wednesday's brief requires 6-9 destroyers and 3-4 mine countermeasure vessels for continuous convoy coverage, has not yet been stood up at scale. The tanker was hit in the interim.

The Kpler vessel tracking data is the most analytically rigorous assessment of the Hormuz situation now in the market. Their conclusion: the Strait is effectively closed to commercial shipping not through a physical blockade but through insurance withdrawal. War risk premiums have rendered the passage economically non-viable for most commercial operators. Iranian and Chinese-flagged vessels continue transiting; everyone else has stopped. This is the mechanism through which oil at $80 WTI is still an underpriced scenario. A formal physical closure of the Strait would be the next escalation tier and would push oil toward the $120-150 range that Kpler cited as a one-day disruption scenario at the start of the week. The effective insurance closure is producing supply disruption at a slower rate but the direction is the same.

Thursday's equity session was a textbook response to an oil shock reaching a psychologically significant price level. WTI crossed $80 intraday and the Dow fell 1,000 points in the same 30-minute window. The correlation was explicit: Dow down 1,000 points at the moment oil hit $80, as Reuters reported. The recovery from those lows to close -1.61% reflected relief as oil pulled back from intraday highs, not any improvement in the geopolitical picture. Small caps closed -1.91%, the worst performer of the session, as rate-sensitive and domestically-focused companies absorbed both the economic slowdown signal and the sustained high-yield environment simultaneously.

The structural exception to Thursday's selloff was Broadcom. AVGO gained 4.8% after reporting AI chip revenue up 74% year-over-year, the strongest single-name earnings signal of the week. The Broadcom result does two things: it confirms that hyperscaler AI capex is not being cut in the current environment, which is a positive data point for Nvidia's forward revenue, and it establishes that the AI infrastructure buildout has its own momentum independent of the geopolitical backdrop. The caveat, as noted earlier this week, is that a significant portion of hyperscaler Gulf infrastructure is now in an active conflict zone. The demand is intact; the geography is stressed. Those are two different risk factors and they are being conflated in the current market narrative.

Iran's foreign minister was direct on Thursday: no ceasefire, no negotiations. The successor government following Khamenei's death is asserting continuity of resistance posture. Dan Niles of Niles Investment Management framed the market's central question precisely on CNBC: short war of under a month means oil normalises and equities recover; medium-term conflict is a larger problem; a prolonged engagement means oil above $100 and probable global recession. That three-scenario framework is now the operative investment decision tree. The current oil price, WTI at $80, Brent at $85, is pricing a conflict somewhere between short and medium-term. The tanker attack on Day 7 is an incremental signal that short-term resolution is not the base case.

Today's Catalyst · Non-Farm Payrolls · February · 08:30 ET

No Clean Read. Three Scenarios, Three Different Market Problems.

Consensus: 58k-65k (vs. 130k January). Unemployment expected: 4.4%. Average hourly earnings: +0.4% month-over-month. This is a pre-conflict February number, meaning it captures the tariff slowdown but not the Iran war. The number's interpretation depends entirely on which risk the market is currently more afraid of: inflation or growth.

NFP Scenario Matrix · March 6, 2026 · 08:30 ET
Scenario A · Strong Beat (>100k) · Bullish for Growth, Bearish for Rates

A big number confirms labor market resilience and reduces recession fear. But in a $80 WTI environment with heating oil up 4% in a single session, a strong jobs print removes the last rationale for Fed cuts entirely. The 2Y would move sharply higher, the curve would flatten further, and rate-sensitive equities (small caps, REITs, homebuilders) would underperform. The "Goldilocks" scenario that equities want does not exist in this inflation context. Strong NFP is good for earnings expectations and bad for the discount rate at the same time.

Scenario B · In-Line (58k-80k) · Ambiguous, Market Leans Green

A result in or slightly above consensus is probably the mildest market mover. It confirms the tariff-driven labor slowdown that has been visible in PMI data without shocking in either direction. Markets likely drift higher on relief that the worst growth fears are not confirmed in the data. This is the setup European futures appear to be pricing with DAX +0.86% and S&P futures +0.10% overnight. The risk is that in-line 60k is read as "already below 100k is weak enough." Context determines interpretation.

Scenario C · Miss (<50k) · Growth Shock Confirmed. Stagflation Completed.

A print below 50k would confirm that the tariff drag was sharper than expected in February, before the Iran shock added the oil cost channel. The stagflation configuration becomes fully explicit: oil at $80, yields rising, labor market cooling, Fed unable to cut. This is the scenario that produces the worst equity outcomes because it means neither the growth nor the inflation side of the equation is benign. Small caps and consumer discretionary bear the heaviest impact. The 2Y might rally briefly on "forced cut" pricing before the inflation channel reasserts itself. This is the scenario Dan Niles' medium-term conflict framework implies is in view.

The Hormuz Picture · Kpler Intelligence
Kpler Vessel Tracking · March 5, 2026 · Effective Closure via Insurance

The Strait of Hormuz is not formally closed. Vessel tracking shows limited traffic continuing: primarily Iranian and Chinese-flagged ships. Commercial operators, major oil companies, and insurers have effectively withdrawn from the corridor. War risk premiums make the passage economically non-viable for most standard commercial voyages.

Saudi Arabia's East-West Pipeline (capacity: 7 million barrels per day) and the UAE's Fujairah pipeline offer partial alternatives, but terminal infrastructure at Jeddah limits throughput. These routes sustain a portion of displaced volume but cannot offset a full effective closure.

The competitive beneficiary: Russia. With Middle East barrels facing logistical disruption, India and China face strong incentives to deepen reliance on Russian crude. India is the most acute near-term case and is likely pivoting immediately. China, which had been moderating Russian crude intake, is expected to reverse that restraint if the conflict extends. The US Treasury has already signalled this dynamic with a 30-day waiver granted Thursday for India's purchase of Russian oil, a tacit acknowledgement that fighting a war on the Iranian energy front while maintaining Russian oil sanctions simultaneously is not operationally coherent.

Investment Theses · Three Frameworks for the Week's Close
Thesis I · The Bond Curve Sends Its Clearest Signal Yet
US 2Y -0.018 Overnight While 30Y +0.007. The Curve Is Steepening. That Is the Bond Market Pricing a Fed Cut It Does Not Want to Make.

The first meaningful overnight pullback in the US 2Y yield, down 1.8 basis points to 3.581%, while the 30Y continues drifting higher to 4.760%, is the most nuanced bond market signal of the week. A steepening yield curve, short end rallying while long end sells, typically reflects a market beginning to price a central bank that will be forced to ease despite inflation concerns. The 2Y pulling back could mean: NFP is expected weak enough that the market is pricing a forced Fed pivot; or; some positioning cleanup ahead of the data. It does not mean the inflation trade is over. The 30Y still rising confirms inflation expectations are sticky at the long end. What is changing is the market's assessment of how much political and economic pressure will eventually force the Fed's hand despite those inflation expectations.

The CME FedWatch data is the cleanest expression of this: less than 3% probability of a March cut, one-in-three chance of any cut by June, and the base case now pushed to September. That September base case with the 2Y at 3.581% and the 30Y at 4.760% implies a 118-basis-point spread that is historically consistent with late-cycle environments where the central bank is behind the curve on easing into a slowdown. The Fed meets March 17-18. Powell's press conference language on the energy-inflation vs. growth-slowdown balance will be the single most market-moving event of the next two weeks, whatever the NFP says this morning.

Thesis II · Broadcom and the Separated AI Thesis
AVGO +4.8% on AI Revenue +74% YoY. The AI Capex Cycle Is Intact. The Gulf Geography Is Not. These Are Two Different Investment Risks.

Broadcom's Thursday earnings result is analytically important for a specific reason: it establishes that hyperscaler AI capital expenditure is continuing at full pace despite the geopolitical backdrop. CEO Hock Tan's 74% year-over-year AI chip revenue growth is not a backward-looking number in the context of this week; it is a forward signal that Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta have not cut their AI infrastructure commitments. That is the demand-side of the AI hardware thesis confirmed into the worst geopolitical week of 2026.

The separated risk is geographic. Three of those five hyperscalers have committed significant infrastructure to the Gulf: AWS in the UAE (already hit in the conflict), Microsoft Azure in Qatar and UAE, Google in Saudi Arabia. The demand for AI compute is unambiguously intact. The question is whether Gulf-based sovereign AI projects from G42, ADQ, Aramco, and NEOM are accelerating or pausing in the current environment, and whether any hyperscaler issues guidance commentary about Gulf infrastructure risk in upcoming earnings calls. Broadcom's strong print does not resolve this question; it sharpens it. The AI infrastructure investment cycle is intact and the geography of that infrastructure is now in an active conflict zone. Those two facts will be reconciled over the next 60-90 days of earnings guidance cycles.

Thesis III · Russia's Quiet Win and the Sanctions Architecture Cracking
US Treasury Granted India a 30-Day Russian Oil Waiver Thursday. The Iran War Is Inadvertently Relieving Pressure on the Russia Sanctions Regime.

The US Treasury's 30-day waiver for India's purchase of Russian oil is one of the most significant policy developments of the week and has received almost no equity market attention. The logic is straightforward: India, which imports a substantial share of its oil through Gulf channels, faces an acute supply problem if Hormuz is effectively closed. Russia is the most immediately available alternative supplier with established logistics to Indian refineries. Sanctioning Indian purchases of Russian oil while simultaneously fighting a war that has closed the primary alternative supply corridor is not operationally sustainable. The waiver is the Treasury acknowledging this.

The second-order effect is the Kpler-identified China dimension. China, which had been moderating its Russian crude intake under diplomatic pressure, now has explicit cover to increase purchases: the supply disruption from Hormuz gives Beijing an economic justification that is indistinguishable from the geopolitical opportunism that Western policymakers would prefer to constrain. The Iran war is inadvertently providing Russia with increased energy revenue at a moment when the sanctions architecture was beginning to materially constrain Kremlin financing. This is the kind of strategic second-order consequence that does not appear in the first week of any major conflict and tends to become visible only after the fact. It is now visible. The Russia-China-India crude triangle is being reconstructed in real time, and the US has just blessed the first transaction.
