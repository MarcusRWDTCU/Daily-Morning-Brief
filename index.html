Analysis

CPI Was Inline. The Relief Lasted 90 Minutes. Iran Then Said "Not One Litre." The IEA Released 400 Million Barrels. Oil Rose Anyway.

This brief wrote yesterday that a cool CPI print would produce "brief relief" with a half-life measured in hours. The February CPI came in exactly inline at +2.4% YoY, core fractionally soft at +0.2% MoM against a +0.3% whisper. Markets got their 90 minutes. Then the IRGC said not one litre of oil would pass through Hormuz. Hegseth called it the most intense day of strikes. Three ships were hit in the Strait. Drones landed near Dubai's airport. The IEA released 400 million barrels, the largest coordinated reserve action in history. Oil closed up 4.5%. The Gilt reversed both recovery sessions in one day. The asymmetric reaction function worked with textbook precision.

The February CPI result itself was clean. Headline at +2.4% YoY, core at +2.5% YoY, shelter rising just 0.1% monthly for its smallest gain since January 2021. The disinflationary momentum in housing that the Fed had been waiting for was present. Tariff impacts on goods were visible but not accelerating. Sonu Varghese at Carson Group summarized the consensus read precisely: this CPI confirms the Fed had an inflation problem before the energy shock, and it is "the calm before the storm." The February print is the last backward-looking data point before the March CPI, which arrives in mid-April and will show oil averaging above $90 for an entire month, pump prices 19% above their pre-conflict level at $3.50 per gallon in the US and approaching €2.00 per liter in Germany, and refinery capacity impaired across three major Gulf complexes. The February number told markets what they already knew. The market moved on within the session.

The mine discovery is the most consequential tactical development of the week and is receiving a fraction of the attention of the IRGC's $200 oil warning. US forces destroyed 16 Iranian minelayers near the Strait of Hormuz on Wednesday, which confirms that Iran has been deploying naval mines in or near the channel. Naval mines are fundamentally different from missile and drone attacks as a maritime threat. Missiles and drones require active launch capability to remain a danger. Mines persist autonomously in the water until swept. The US Navy's convoy escort pledge, the G7 SPR releases, and the IEA coordinated action all address the threat posed by active Iranian fire. None of them clear mines. Dedicated minesweeping operations require MCM vessels, careful navigation lanes, and significant time. The Fifth Fleet's normal Gulf posture includes a handful of dedicated minesweeping assets. The Strait of Hormuz is 21 miles wide at its narrowest point. The number of mine-capable lanes through that navigation channel is finite and small. If Iran has seeded those lanes, resuming commercial transit requires confirming they are clear, which is a physical operation that takes days to weeks even with dedicated assets fully deployed. The IEA can release 400 million barrels. It cannot sweep mines.

The IEA release itself deserves an honest accounting of what it can and cannot do. Four hundred million barrels is the largest coordinated emergency reserve release in history, exceeding even the volumes deployed after Hurricane Katrina and during the early phase of the Ukraine war. At current global consumption of approximately 103 million barrels per day, 400 million barrels represents roughly four days of total global oil demand. The relevant metric is not total consumption but the lost Hormuz throughput. Before the conflict, approximately 20 million barrels per day transited the Strait. That flow has effectively stopped. Four hundred million barrels provides the equivalent of 20 days of Hormuz-equivalent supply at pre-closure flow rates, or roughly three weeks of buffer against the physical disruption if drawn down at the pace needed. The energy market analyst at Marex was direct: the IEA release "buys us a few days," and "this conflict needs to end by the end of the week, otherwise we'll see oil prices spike back above $100." The conflict did not end by the end of the week. Oil is at $92.55 pre-market. The clock on the IEA buffer has started.

The UAE dimension entered the picture directly on Wednesday. Dubai airport is the world's second busiest international hub and serves as the primary connection point for Gulf carrier traffic between Asia, Europe, and the Americas. Drones falling in the vicinity of its airspace is not a peripheral event for energy markets. The UAE is the Gulf's largest non-Saudi refined products re-export hub and the location of the Fujairah oil terminal, which this brief has tracked as the primary alternative export route for Gulf barrels that cannot transit Hormuz. If the conflict reaches the point where UAE airspace and infrastructure are operationally threatened, the alternative routing thesis that has partially supported oil prices below their $119 crisis peak begins to close off. For European markets, the UAE disruption dimension matters because Gulf aviation connectivity runs through Dubai. Lufthansa, Air France, and the major European carriers all have significant UAE transit exposure. Higher jet fuel costs plus reduced connectivity to Asian markets via Dubai adds a second-order economic cost to European corporates that has not yet been quantified in the data.

Germany and Europe's gasoline market is absorbing the re-escalation in real time. The ADAC national average for Super E10 had already climbed above €1.90 per liter by early March, approaching the all-time German record of €2.259 set in March 2022. Wednesday's oil re-escalation, with WTI up more than 6% and Heating Oil up 2.71%, will translate into a further German and EU pump price increase within the weekly ADAC reporting cycle. The EU average was €1.574 per liter as of March 2, meaning German consumers are already paying approximately 25% above the continental average and rising. The political context matters: Germany is in the early stages of a new coalition government, navigating a fiscal framework under the recently revised debt brake, and facing a consumer confidence environment that was already fragile before €2.00 per liter at the forecourt became the reference price. The Carson Group's "calm before the storm" framing applies as precisely to German CPI as to US CPI. Germany's February inflation came in around 2.1% YoY. The March reading, shaped by the same structural energy shock, will be a materially different number.

Three Theses · What the Data Is Actually Saying
Thesis I · The Mine Thesis
US Forces Destroyed 16 Iranian Minelayers. SPR Releases Cannot Sweep Mines. This Is the Threat That Persists After Every Diplomatic or Market Intervention.

Naval mines represent a qualitatively different threat to Hormuz transit than missiles, drones, or gunboat harassment. Missiles and drones require an active Iranian decision to fire and can be addressed through air defense, escort operations, or deterrence. Mines, once deployed, are autonomous. They do not require an Iranian soldier to press a button. They persist in the water until physically removed by dedicated MCM vessels operating in cleared lanes. The discovery that Iran was seeding the Strait with mines is the strategic development that the market has underpriced relative to the IRGC's $200 price warning, which is the headline number drawing attention. A $200 oil forecast is a threat about what might happen. Naval mines in the Strait are an operational fact about what is already there. Every diplomatic resolution framework, every SPR release, and every escort pledge must now include a mine clearance operation as a precondition for any resumption of commercial transit. This adds weeks to any realistic reopening timeline regardless of whether Iran agrees to stop active attacks. The IEA's 400 million barrels buys approximately 20 days of Hormuz-equivalent supply. Mine clearance takes longer than 20 days.

Thesis II · The UK Gilt as Structural Barometer
The Gilt Fell 1.53% Wednesday, Erasing Both Recovery Sessions. Cumulative Decline Back Above 7%. The Brief Stabilization Was the Relief Trade. This Is the Structure.

The UK Gilt's behavior across the past four sessions carries more information than any single session's move. From Day 9, the Gilt had declined seven consecutive sessions for a cumulative loss of approximately 7.4% from pre-crisis levels. Then it recovered 0.52% on Day 10 and 0.79% on Day 11, partially on the oil retreat from $119 and partially on the CPI relief trade. Wednesday it fell 1.53%, erasing both recovery sessions in a single day and returning the cumulative decline back above 7% from pre-crisis. The pattern is precise: when oil retreats on verbal interventions or data-driven relief, the Gilt stabilizes. When operational facts reassert, the Gilt resumes its structural decline. This is the cleanest expression in the bond market of the difference between the verbal narrative and the physical reality of the conflict. The Gilt is not pricing day-to-day oil moves. It is pricing the stagflation scenario that this brief has documented since Day 2: a central bank that cannot cut into an oil shock and cannot raise into a contracting labor market, holding a fixed policy rate while both mandates deteriorate simultaneously. Wednesday's Gilt move is not a new development. It is a return to the structure after a two-day interruption.

Thesis III · Germany and Europe Entering the Pump Price Crisis Zone
Brent Back Toward $100. German Pump Already Near €1.97/L. The March 2022 All-time Record of €2.259/L Is Now the Reference Point for the Scenario Analysis.

With Brent at $97.78 and rising, the German pump price trajectory is accelerating back toward €2.00 per liter and beyond. The ADAC national average was at approximately €1.897 per liter as of early March, up 12.1 cents in a single week, with individual stations in Bremen and other German cities already crossing €2.00. The 2022 record of €2.259 was set when Brent averaged approximately €110 per barrel at peak Ukraine-shock pricing. Brent is now at $97.78 pre-market and has touched $101 intraday within the past week. The EU average was €1.574 as of March 2, meaning Germany is already well above the continental average. European consumers face a structural pump price crisis that is being built in real time from three compounding factors: the crude shock, the refinery capacity offline at Gulf complexes, and the LNG disruption that adds to European natural gas costs through a separate supply channel. The DAX and Euro Stoxx 50 both entering Thursday in the red, with futures down 0.80% and 0.81% respectively, is the equity market beginning to price the compound nature of Europe's exposure rather than treating it as a single variable. German and European household disposable income is being compressed at the pump. Consumer-facing sectors, retail, and anything with a transport cost component will show this in the Q1 earnings reports that begin arriving in April.
