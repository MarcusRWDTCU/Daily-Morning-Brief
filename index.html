Analysis

Nvidia Fell 5% on the Best Earnings in Tech History. The 10-Year Broke 4%. Something Has Changed.

Nvidia reported $68.13B in revenue, guided to $78B for Q1, and its stock fell 5.5% on Thursday, its worst day since April. The US 10-year yield broke below 4% for the first time since November 28. Dell reported a quarter of historic magnitude and barely moved futures. The market is telling us something about where we are in this cycle.

Open Range Capital Research · Marcus Drauschke · Feb 27, 2026

Nvidia beat every number the market had. Revenue of $68.13B against a $66.21B consensus, earnings of $1.62 against a $1.53 estimate, and a Q1 FY2027 guide of approximately $78B against a $72.8B Street estimate: a 7% beat on forward guidance. Jensen Huang announced that Vera Rubin samples had shipped to customers ahead of schedule. The data center business generated $62.3B, up 75% year over year. By any conventional standard, this was a historic quarter.

The stock fell 5.5% on Thursday. Its worst day since April 2025. Other chip stocks followed: Broadcom, Lam Research, Western Digital, and Applied Materials all sold off. The Nasdaq closed down 1.18% at 22,878. The S&P 500 fell 0.54% to 6,908. The Dow, shielded from chip exposure by its industrial and financial composition, gained 0.03%. Motley Fool put it accurately: the majority of stocks in the S&P 500 actually rose on Thursday. Nvidia's index weight was large enough to drag the market down regardless.

The more significant signal came from bonds. The US 10-year yield closed Thursday at 3.998%, below 4% for the first time since November 28. The 2-year fell 2.8 basis points to 3.420%. Rates are falling as chips sell off. That convergence suggests something beyond mechanical profit-taking: the bond market is beginning to price slower growth. Whether that is a rotation-driven technical move or a genuine repricing of the economic outlook is the central question entering Friday. Today's PPI at 08:30 ET will be the first data point toward an answer.

Huang pushed back directly in a CNBC interview Thursday, saying "I think the markets got it wrong" on software disruption. He defended ServiceNow by name, arguing that AI agents are fine-tuned tools that augment enterprise software rather than replace it. It is notable that the CEO of the world's most valuable AI infrastructure company felt compelled to defend the application layer. Huang understands that a prolonged SaaS collapse reduces enterprise IT budgets: the same budgets that fund Nvidia chip purchases.

Dell Technologies reported after the close Thursday in what should have been an unambiguously positive catalyst for the infrastructure thesis: Q4 revenue of $33.4B against a $31.75B consensus, EPS of $3.89 against a $3.44 estimate, ISG (data center and AI servers) up 73% year over year, and FY2027 guidance of $138-142B in revenue and $12.90 in EPS against a prior consensus near $10.56. A 22% upside surprise on forward earnings guidance. Futures are still lower this morning. The market is not rewarding strong fundamentals right now. It is processing fear.

Investment Theses · Three Positions to Watch
Thesis I · The 10-Year Below 4%
Rates Are Telling a Different Story Than Equities

The US 10-year closed Thursday at 3.998%, below 4% for the first time since November 28. The 2-year fell to 3.420%, pricing additional Fed easing. Two readings are possible. First, investors are mechanically rotating from equities into Treasuries, and the bond rally is a consequence of equity selling rather than an independent growth signal. Second, the bond market is independently pricing a slowdown: high-profile tech layoffs, tariff uncertainty, and softening consumer sentiment are registering in growth expectations.

The 2-year is the tell. At 3.420% and falling, it means the market is pricing more rate cuts than a week ago. If that continues through today's data, it is not just a positioning flush. PPI at 08:30 ET is the first test. A miss on PPI would accelerate the rate rally and put further pressure on equities. A beat would push back against the growth-slowdown thesis and potentially provide a floor for stocks.

Thesis II · Commodities Resilience
Silver +3.81%. Copper +1.42%. The Anti-Dollar Trade Is Not Finished.

Silver bounced sharply overnight to $90.31 (+3.81%), recovering Wednesday's pullback entirely. Copper climbed to $6.1107 (+1.42%), approaching multi-year highs. Gold holds at $5,203 (+0.18%). These moves are happening against falling US yields, a weaker dollar (EUR/USD above 1.18), and continued tariff uncertainty. The commodity complex is not pricing recession. It is pricing sustained inflation, dollar debasement, and structural supply constraints. Copper's strength is a forward-looking signal on industrial demand that sits in direct tension with the recessionary read from falling bond yields. The market is running two contradictory macro pictures simultaneously. Both cannot be right.

The JGB reversal overnight adds context. Japan 10Y fell 1.72%, 30Y fell 1.24%, 40Y fell 1.49%. Wednesday's spike is fully reversing. This reduces near-term pressure on Japanese institutional capital repatriation, which is a mild global positive for Treasuries and European bonds.

Thesis III · The SaaS Question After Huang's Intervention
The Most Credible Voice in AI Just Defended the Application Layer

Jensen Huang's CNBC comment is the most significant SaaS-sector development of the week that did not involve an earnings report. His argument, that AI agents work with enterprise software rather than replacing it, is the bull case for CRM, WDAY, and NOW in a single sentence from the person most qualified to make it. Fundstrat's head of technical strategy separately said Salesforce "looks poised to bounce in the weeks ahead." Snowflake rose 2.3% Thursday after Citi raised its price target to $280, citing AI momentum.

None of this settles the structural question. IGV is still down more than 10% in February. Workday remains sharply lower. The thesis in our Part I and Part II analysis argued that the disruption risk is real and underpriced. Huang's intervention sets a narrative floor, not a structural one. The data will settle this over the next two to three earnings cycles. His quote is a starting point for mean reversion, not a resolution.

Today's Setup

Month-End Friday With PPI and a Market That Is Processing Fear

Today is the last trading day of February. Month-end rebalancing is a real mechanical force: pension funds and balanced funds that have seen bond allocations shrink relative to equities during February's tech selloff may be forced sellers of equities and buyers of bonds today, regardless of the news flow. This would extend the rate rally and add pressure to stocks at the open.

PPI at 08:30 is the morning's primary data point. The January print reflects pre-tariff conditions, which means any upside surprise indicates inflation was already accelerating before the 15% levy. That combination, pre-existing inflation plus incoming tariff-driven cost pressures, would be the most uncomfortable scenario for the Fed: stagflationary pressure with a slowing growth picture. A downside print would confirm the bond market's growth-slowdown thesis and likely accelerate the Treasury rally.

Consumer sentiment at 10:00 rounds out the picture. If the Michigan final print has deteriorated further since the preliminary reading, it adds to the weight of evidence that the tariff uncertainty and tech sector anxiety are feeding through to the consumer. Watch for the tariff section of the sentiment survey specifically; anecdotal reports suggest tariff-related anxiety is now showing up in household survey responses for the first time.
