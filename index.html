The more unusual overnight development is in Japanese government bonds. The Japan 10Y yield is at 2.126%, up 1.82% on the session. The 30Y is at 3.369%, up 2.78%. The 40Y is at 3.612%, up 2.50%. These are large moves for a market not known for volatility. The Bank of Japan has been gradually tightening, but a move of this size in the long end suggests either forced selling, a policy signal, or spillover from global fiscal anxiety. Japanese yields at these levels matter for global capital allocation: as JGB yields rise, Japanese institutional investors (among the largest holders of US Treasuries and European bonds) have less incentive to seek yield abroad. Watch this.

Against this backdrop, tonight is Nvidia. Futures are flat partly because the market is holding its breath. The question is Jensen Huang's forward guidance: Blackwell ramp trajectory, data center demand visibility, and any comment on tariff exposure to Taiwan supply chains. Salesforce and Snowflake also report after the close, both real-time tests of whether the SaaSpocalypse narrative is priced or just getting started.

The structural trade here remains unchanged: the Section 122 authority expires in 147 days, Congress has shown no urgency to act, and the EU has publicly rejected renegotiation. Gold at $5,210 and silver at $90.42 are the market's live verdict on this thesis. Both metals are pricing sustained dollar debasement and tariff-driven inflation. The anti-dollar, anti-Treasury trade is intact.

Macro Note

Cook's Warning: The Fed May Be Structurally Locked Out of the Next Downturn

Speaking Tuesday at the National Association for Business Economics, Fed Governor Lisa Cook made an observation that deserves more attention than it has received. She argued that an AI-driven productivity boom could raise unemployment without that unemployment representing genuine demand-side slack, a condition where the Fed's standard cure of rate cuts would be inflationary rather than corrective. Simply put: if AI destroys jobs while boosting productivity and output, the labor market deteriorates without the indicators that normally justify easing. The Fed would be fighting a different kind of problem with the wrong tool.

Rate markets are still pricing in two 25-basis-point cuts for 2026. If Cook's framework gains traction within the FOMC, that pricing is wrong: not because rates go higher, but because the trigger for cuts may never arrive in the expected form. That is a significant tail risk sitting inside the "Fed put will save us" assumption that underpins most equity bull case scenarios. Watch today's Fed speakers for any corroboration of Cook's framing.
