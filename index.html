Analysis

Trump Said Three Words. Oil Fell 25%. Gold Went Up. The Strait Is Still Closed.

The script from Day 3 repeated itself on Day 10 with near-perfect precision. A Trump verbal intervention collapsed oil from $119 to $89 in the same session. Equities closed green. VIX dropped 13.5%. Bonds rallied. The relief trade ran exactly as it ran when he posted "FREE FLOW OF ENERGY" ten days ago. One asset did not follow the script: gold rose 1.60% to $5,185, reaching a new level even as the supposed resolution narrative played out in crude. The market that prices geopolitical risk with the longest memory did not sell. That is the signal that matters this morning.

Open Range Capital Research · Marcus Drauschke · March 10, 2026

The structure of Monday is identical to Day 3. A Trump statement produces a 25-30% intraday oil swing, equities recover from the lows, VIX compresses, and the relief trade prints a green close. On Day 3, the S&P recovered 160 points on the escort pledge. On Day 10, it closed +0.83% on a CBS phone interview. In both cases, the relief was real and the move was tradeable. In neither case did it resolve the operational problem. The Strait of Hormuz was still closed after the escort pledge. It is still closed now. Iran said explicitly on Monday that there is "no point to talk about anything but defense and retaliation as long as attacks continue." The G7 discussed strategic reserves. The US Embassy in Riyadh began evacuating non-essential staff. New Israeli strikes hit Iranian energy infrastructure. The conflict is simultaneously generating relief rally language and escalatory operational facts, and Monday's session compressed both into a single 25% intraday oil range.

The gold signal is the most analytically important data point from Monday and is receiving the least discussion. WTI fell 25% from its intraday high. Brent fell 25% from its intraday high. Gold finished the session up 1.60% to $5,185. Silver, which has been the growth-sensitive counterpart to gold's pure safe-haven positioning throughout this conflict, rose 5.52%. If the market genuinely believed that Trump's CBS statement represented a credible path toward Hormuz reopening and conflict de-escalation, the gold position would have been the first trade to reverse. It is the classic geopolitical premium asset. When geopolitical risk recedes, gold gives back the premium. It did not give back anything on Monday. It added to its position at a new high. The gold market is telling you that it is pricing Trump's words the same way it priced the escort pledge: as a verbal commitment without operational confirmation, not as evidence of actual resolution.

The heating oil divergence is the second signal that the crude market is not fully pricing the damage correctly. WTI fell 5.52% in the overnight continuation. Heating Oil is up 6.51%. Gasoline is down 2.38%. This is not a unified energy market pricing a consistent outlook. Crude is responding to Trump's verbal intervention and the G7 SPR signal, both of which address the logistics of moving oil through Hormuz. Heating Oil is responding to something different: the destruction of refining capacity. Ras Tanura is offline. Bapco has declared force majeure. Ras Laffan has declared force majeure. These are not tankers waiting for escort. These are physical refining and processing facilities that have been struck or shuttered. You can reopen a strait in days if the political conditions are met. You cannot restart a refinery in days under any conditions. The market that prices refined products is pricing the refinery damage. The market that prices crude is pricing Trump. They are not pricing the same thing, and the divergence between heating oil and crude is the most structurally important number in today's data.

The equity recovery deserves an honest accounting. Monday closed green across US indices and the relief was real: S&P +0.83%, Nasdaq +1.38%, VIX from an intraday print of 35.30 down to 25.50 at the close. These are genuine moves. The caveat is the intraday range. The Dow's session low was 46,615. Its session high was 47,876. A 1,261-point range in a single session on a day when the index closed up 239 points is not a stable equity market. It is a market that is extremely sensitive to single-statement interventions and is using each Trump comment as the organizing variable for direction. That works until it does not, and the condition under which it stops working is the moment when the operational facts on the ground diverge too visibly from the verbal narrative. The Strait remains closed. Iran is not negotiating. Iraq has cut 70% of its production. Saudi Aramco is shutting in fields. The gap between Trump's "war is very complete, pretty much" and the actual state of the Gulf energy system is wide enough to be seen clearly from every data point in this morning's sheet.

The crude oil market and the refined products market are currently pricing two different realities. WTI responded to Trump's CBS interview and the G7 SPR announcement by falling sharply, pricing an improvement in the probability that Hormuz reopens and barrels begin moving again. Heating Oil moved in the opposite direction. The divergence is explained by the refinery damage that has accumulated across ten days of conflict and is not addressed by any verbal intervention, SPR release, or naval escort pledge.

Ras Tanura, Saudi Arabia's largest refinery and among the largest in the world, is offline. Bapco Energies in Bahrain has declared force majeure. Qatar's Ras Laffan complex, which is simultaneously the world's largest LNG production facility and a major refined products hub, has declared force majeure. These are not tankers waiting for an insurance guarantee before they transit the Strait. These are physical processing facilities that have been struck, damaged, or shuttered as precautionary measures under missile and drone threat. A refinery that has been hit cannot be restarted by a statement to CBS News. It requires physical inspection, repair, safety certification, and a full restart sequence that takes weeks to months depending on the nature of the damage. The refined products market understands this timeline. The crude market is pricing the verbal commitment. The pump price at the forecourt, which is set by refined products not crude, is going to be driven by Heating Oil's reality, not WTI's hope.

The national average gasoline price in the United States reached $3.478 per gallon as of Monday, up 16% from the prior week. In Germany, the ADAC national average for Super E10 stood at €1.897 per liter as of March 3, a jump of 12.1 cents in a single week, with the trend reading from tankpreise.de showing the national average approaching €1.973 by March 9. Individual stations in Bremen and other German cities have already crossed the €2.00 mark. For context, the German all-time record is €2.259 per liter, set in March 2022 during the Russia-Ukraine energy shock. The EU-wide average was €1.574 per liter as of March 2, meaning Germany is now approximately 25% above the European average and accelerating. The last time German pump prices moved this fast was the week after Russia invaded Ukraine. That episode produced a political crisis across every major European government. This one is arriving faster because the refinery damage layer is compounding the transit disruption rather than running in parallel with it.

The DAX's -0.77% Monday close, diverging from the US green session, reflects a different energy exposure. Europe imports all of its oil and has no strategic reserve intervention comparable to the US program. The ECB is in the same policy trap as the Fed but without the dollar's reserve currency insulation. German industrial companies, which anchor the DAX, are pricing energy at the refined products level rather than the relief-trade WTI close. The Heating Oil move this morning continues to add to the pressure that will drive the March and April CPI prints in both Europe and the US. Tomorrow's CPI release covers February, before this refinery damage existed. March CPI, arriving in mid-April, will be the first number to fully capture it.

Three Theses · What the Data Is Actually Saying
Thesis I · The Pattern Has a Name
This Is the Second Time Trump Has Moved Oil 25% Intraday With a Single Statement. The Escort Pledge on Day 3 Did Not Reopen the Strait. Neither Did This.

Day 3 produced the same sequence: Trump posted "FREE FLOW OF ENERGY" and S&P futures recovered 160 points while oil pulled back sharply. This publication ran the convoy math showing why the escort pledge was logistically incomplete. The Strait did not reopen. On Day 10, Trump told CBS the war is "very complete, pretty much" and WTI fell from $119 to $89 in the same session. The verbal pattern is now established: Trump makes a statement, oil falls, equities rally, and the operational reality of the Strait does not change. This playbook has a finite number of uses. The market continues to respond to it because each statement could theoretically be the one that precedes actual resolution. But each time it does not produce resolution, the credibility capital of the next statement is slightly lower. The current pricing is: equities trading hope, gold trading reality.

Thesis II · CPI Tomorrow Is Backward-Looking. March CPI Is the Real Number.
Tomorrow's February CPI Captures a World Where Oil Was $65-70. The Number That Matters Arrives in April.

February CPI, released tomorrow at 08:30 ET, captures the pre-conflict inflation environment. Oil averaged approximately $65-70 in February before the strikes on February 28. The tariff effect from January and February will show up. The Iran shock will not. A hot February CPI print will accelerate bond selling and add pressure on the Fed. A cool print will produce temporary relief but will not change the trajectory because the March data is already building toward a dramatically different number. By the time March CPI reports in mid-April, oil will have averaged well above $100 for the entire month of March, gasoline retail will have risen 16% or more, heating oil will have added further, and the refinery damage will have propagated into product prices. The bond market understands this distinction and is likely to trade the February number with a shorter half-life than it would in a normal environment.

Thesis III · Bitcoin Is Now 10 Days Into the Geopolitical Hedge Trade
Bitcoin +4.26% on Monday While Oil Fell 25% Intraday. It Has Outperformed Every Major Equity Index for Ten Consecutive Sessions.

The Bitcoin geopolitical hedge thesis identified in this brief on Day 4 has now produced ten consecutive sessions of documented outperformance versus all major equity indices. Monday's +4.26% came on the same session that S&P printed +0.83% and DAX printed -0.77%. Bitcoin is not trading with the relief rally and it is not trading with the risk-off. It is trading as a store of value that is independent of the resolution narrative. Gold is doing the same thing with greater institutional recognition. The two assets most cleanly expressing the view that this conflict is not resolved, regardless of what the verbal narrative says, are gold and Bitcoin. Both rose on Monday. Neither is priced for the war being "very complete, pretty much." The equity market is. The gap between those two sets of prices is the trade that the next operational development will settle.
