Geopolitical Event · Weekend Development
Joint US-Israel military strikes on Iran were executed over the weekend of February 28-March 1, 2026. Oil markets opened sharply higher. Gold broke to a new all-time high. Equity futures are pricing a significant risk-off open for Monday.

The War Premium Has Arrived. Gold at a New Record, Oil at $72, and Every Risk Asset Repricing in Real Time.

Over the weekend, US-Israeli strikes on Iranian nuclear and military infrastructure triggered the supply disruption scenario that oil markets had been pricing as a tail risk for weeks. WTI is up 8.24% to $72.54. Brent is up 8.77% to $79.26. Gold has broken its January all-time high of $5,364, printing a new record at $5,402. US equity futures are down sharply. European futures are down harder. Monday opens with a new macro regime.

Open Range Capital Research · Marcus Drauschke · March 2, 2026

The Strait of Hormuz is the fulcrum of the global oil market. Approximately 20% of all seaborne oil and refined products passes through this 33-kilometer chokepoint between Oman and Iran. Every barrel exported from Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Iraq, Kuwait, and Iran itself transits this waterway. Analysts at Kpler have estimated that a single day of disruption could push oil to $120-150 per barrel. Barclays has projected Brent at $80 in scenarios of material supply interruption, with prolonged conflict risking $100 or above. We are not at disruption. We are at the risk premium that precedes it.

WTI opened Sunday night at $72.54, up 8.24% from Friday's close of $65.71. Brent is at $79.26, up 8.77%. Heating Oil futures are up 9.35%. Gasoline RBOB is up 5.01%. These are not technical moves or positioning squeezes. They are markets rapidly repricing a genuine supply disruption scenario at the Strait, one that was theoretical on Friday and is no longer theoretical as of Sunday night. Whether Iranian retaliation materializes, and whether it targets the Strait, remains the central unknown. Until that question resolves, the war premium will not come out of oil prices.

Gold has broken to a new all-time high of $5,402.94, surpassing the previous record of $5,364 set on January 29. The move is +$155.04 on the session (+2.95%), the largest single-session gain in weeks. This is not merely an inflation hedge. Gold is repricing three things simultaneously: the energy shock feeding through to CPI, the geopolitical risk premium for a potential escalation to a broader regional conflict, and the continued structural dollar weakness that has been the underlying bid for precious metals since late 2025. Silver is up 2.21% to $95.35. Copper is essentially flat (+0.12%), signaling that the market is not yet pricing an industrial demand surge, just a safety flight.

Equity futures tell the other side. S&P -1.29% to 6,789. Nasdaq -1.63% to 24,553. Dow -1.26% to 48,360. Small Cap 2000 is the sharpest decliner at -1.72% to 2,587. European futures are worse: DAX -2.17%, CAC -1.75%, Euro Stoxx 50 -2.10%. FTSE 100 is notably more resilient at -0.07%, reflecting its significant weighting in energy and materials names that benefit from higher oil. VIX futures are at 22.03, up 6.75%. This is not a panic print, but it is a clear escalation from last week's already-elevated levels. The market is entering a new volatility regime.

The dollar is strengthening on safe-haven flows: EUR/USD fell to 1.1728 (-0.72%), GBP/USD to 1.3373 (-0.84%), AUD/USD to 0.7065 (-0.70%). USD/JPY rose to 157.01 (+0.61%), a notable reversal given the yen's typical safe-haven role; the yen is losing ground because Japan imports nearly all of its oil, making a supply shock structurally negative for the Japanese current account. The US dollar is absorbing the safe-haven bid that would normally flow to both the dollar and the yen, because the yen's oil import exposure overrides its safe-haven status in this specific scenario.

US Treasury yields are ticking up slightly: 10Y at 3.962% (+0.4bp), 30Y at 4.647% (+1.4bp), 2Y at 3.389% (+1.0bp). This is the most counterintuitive read in the data. In a standard risk-off event, yields fall as capital flows into Treasuries. Here, yields are rising modestly because the oil shock is being read as inflationary, and an inflationary supply shock is negative for Treasuries. European bonds, by contrast, are rallying: Bund up 0.44%, UK Gilt up 0.57%. European investors are pricing a growth shock more than an inflation shock. Two different bond markets, two different macro frameworks, one weekend.

Investment Theses · Three Positions for a New Macro Regime
Thesis I · The Strait of Hormuz and the Oil Price Ceiling
$72 Is the Risk Premium. $100+ Is the Disruption Scenario.

WTI at $72.54 and Brent at $79.26 represent the market's initial pricing of a supply disruption risk, not an actual disruption. The Strait of Hormuz handles roughly 20% of global seaborne oil. Iran has historically used Hormuz as a retaliatory lever, and any military action that threatens Iranian territorial waters introduces a non-zero probability of tanker interdiction or mining. Analysts at Kpler put the one-day disruption scenario at $120-150. Barclays projects $80 Brent on material disruption, $100+ on prolonged conflict.

The current level reflects neither outcome. It reflects probability-weighted fear. If diplomatic back-channels open and strikes prove limited in scope, the war premium comes out quickly; oil fell sharply after each prior Israel-Iran exchange in 2025. If retaliation materializes or the Strait is threatened, the upside is severe and asymmetric. For portfolios: energy names are the direct beneficiary, but the secondary effect on inflation expectations may be the more durable trade. WTI at $72 with a 15% tariff regime already in place is a stagflationary combination that the Fed has no clean answer for.

Thesis II · Gold at a New All-Time High
$5,402: Three Bids in One Trade

Gold's new record at $5,402.94 is not a single trade thesis. It is three overlapping macro arguments arriving simultaneously. First: geopolitical flight to safety, the most immediate driver of this weekend's move. Second: inflation expectations repricing upward as an oil shock layered on top of existing tariff-driven cost pressures resets the CPI trajectory. Third: the structural dollar diversification bid that has been building since late 2025, as central banks continue rotating reserves away from US Treasuries. All three are active simultaneously.

Silver at $95.35 (+2.21%) and copper effectively flat (+0.12%) tell the internal story of this gold move: it is safety-driven, not industrial. Silver moves with gold in geopolitical spikes; copper does not, because copper prices industrial demand expectations that an oil shock does not benefit. The precious metals thesis in this publication has been consistent since late February: the anti-dollar, anti-tariff trade is structural. This weekend's event is an accelerant, not a change in direction.

Thesis III · The Stagflation Trap
Oil at $72, Tariffs at 15%, Tech in Correction: The Fed Has No Good Move.

The macroeconomic setup entering Monday is the most challenging combination since 2022. Oil at $72 (and rising) means energy-driven CPI acceleration is coming. The existing 15% tariff regime has already elevated goods inflation. US bond yields are rising on inflation fears rather than falling on growth fears, which is the stagflation signal: the bond market is prioritizing the inflation risk over the growth-slowdown risk that drove last week's rally in Treasuries. If PPI Friday was already running hot, today's oil shock makes the February CPI print, due in about ten days, a potential significant catalyst.

The Fed cannot cut into an oil shock. It cannot hike into a tech correction and slowing growth. The tool for managing a supply-side inflation shock does not exist within the Fed's standard policy framework; rate hikes would accelerate the growth slowdown without solving the supply constraint. This is why US equity futures are down harder than the oil move alone would justify. The market is pricing the policy trap, not just the oil price.

Opening Setup · What to Watch at the Bell

The First Hour Will Define the Week

Monday's open will be the first liquid US price discovery since the strikes were confirmed. Futures pricing at 02:00 ET is an early signal, but the institutional response in the first 60 minutes of cash trading will determine whether this is a one-session risk-off event or the beginning of a sustained repricing. Three things to watch specifically at the open: energy sector performance (XLE, XOM, CVX) as the direct beneficiary and early tell on whether the market believes $72 is a floor or a ceiling; defense sector (RTX, LMT, NOC) as a secondary geopolitical trade; and safe-haven Treasuries for the first real test of whether inflation or growth dominates the market's macro framework for this event.

ISM Manufacturing at 10:00 ET is the day's key macro data point, but in the current environment, a soft print would be absorbed differently than usual: a manufacturing contraction signal on top of an oil shock does not generate a rates rally, it generates concern about stagflation. Any reading below 50 should be watched carefully for its bond market impact rather than its equity market impact.

OPEC+ is the wildcard. Saudi Arabia has been preemptively raising exports in anticipation of potential Hormuz disruption. If OPEC+ makes an emergency statement about supply floor guarantees, it caps the oil upside and potentially provides a floor for equities. If OPEC+ is silent, or if any member signals production cuts in solidarity with Iran, the oil move accelerates materially.
